\begin{abstract}
Planetary rover operations must prioritize scientifically valuable targets under strict bandwidth/energy budgets and delayed ground feedback. We present ARTPS, a modular workflow that combines monocular depth estimation, depth-guided adaptive image enhancement, multi-cue anomaly detection (image, depth, and optional learned models), entropy-weighted dynamic fusion, robust localization, and a learnable curiosity score calibrated via regularized learning. To improve operational reliability, ARTPS uses a soft similarity penalty, feature-space clustering to enforce morphological diversity, and a priority buffer that supports second-chance re-evaluation of high-raw-score targets suppressed by diversity policy. We evaluate anomaly discrimination (AUROC/AUPRC/F1/FPR), depth estimation (RAE, RMSE, MAE, Log10, \(\delta\)-accuracy), and ranking quality (nDCG, Spearman, Kendall). Results indicate improved small-near sensitivity, preserved far-field detail, and reduced false alarms in shadow/specular and low-texture conditions. The paper provides pseudocode, implementation details, and ablations to support reproducibility.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Target selection and sequencing in planetary exploration determine the trade-off between limited acquisition/downlink budgets and scientific return. Communication latency and intermittent links motivate increased on-site autonomy for robotic rovers. In this setting, \textbf{target prioritization} and \textbf{task scheduling} benefit from an end-to-end field intelligence stack driven by perceptual cues. Operational constraints include bandwidth-limited telemetry, energy budgets, thermal limits, and compute/memory ceilings.

In field conditions, “interestingness” cannot be reduced to a single \emph{anomaly} signal. A candidate may be geologically meaningful (e.g., layering, sedimentary structures) and/or valuable under contextual cues (local slope, surface roughness, depth discontinuities). Therefore, a balanced combination of \emph{known value} and \emph{novelty/anomaly} cues yields more consistent scientific return.

ARTPS integrates monocular depth estimation with multi-source anomaly cues to produce \emph{curiosity}-driven ranking. The design goals are: (i) high sensitivity to small near-field targets, (ii) preservation of far-field detail, (iii) suppression of field-specific confounders such as shadows, specularity, and low texture, (iv) explainable outputs via visual overlays and diagnostics, and (v) efficiency suitable for edge deployment. The software architecture follows a modular design with explicit interfaces to support profiling, ablation, and reproducibility.

\section{Related Work}
\textbf{Monocular depth estimation:} Transformer-based architectures and zero-shot cross-dataset transfer have become common for dense depth prediction \cite{dosovitskiy2021image,liu2021swin,ranftl2020towards}. Post-processing such as edge-guided refinement and global smoothing can improve geometric consistency and preserve thin structures, but deployment in field imagery requires careful handling of photometric shifts, perspective effects, and multi-scale texture loss.

\textbf{Industrial visual anomaly detection:} Approaches range from reconstruction-based autoencoders to feature-statistics and patch-distribution modeling \cite{defard2021padim} and memory-bank / nearest-neighbor methods \cite{roth2021towards}. Multi-scale gradients and filtering can enhance details, while low-variance regions and shadow/specular artifacts must be controlled to avoid false alarms.

\textbf{Multi-cue fusion and localization:} Normalization followed by weighted fusion is a practical strategy for combining heterogeneous anomaly cues. Robust localization typically relies on contour extraction, IoU-based non-maximum suppression, and geometric box merging. Fusion quality depends on scale alignment and context-aware weighting.

\textbf{Curiosity-driven exploration:} To maximize scientific return, ranking policies balance \emph{known value} against \emph{novelty/anomaly} and increasingly incorporate learnable weighting, explainability, and uncertainty indicators for safe operator-in-the-loop operation.

\section{Method}
ARTPS is designed as a hybrid workflow that integrates complementary signals from RGB appearance and monocular depth to produce reliable target prioritization under field constraints. All components are modularized for edge execution and profiled for memory footprint, runtime, and numerical stability. At a high level, ARTPS performs preprocessing and depth estimation, extracts multiple anomaly maps, dynamically fuses them, localizes candidate regions, and prioritizes targets with a learnable curiosity policy augmented with diversity and safety mechanisms. Algorithm~\ref{alg:artps} summarizes the end-to-end workflow.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\DontPrintSemicolon
\SetKwInput{KwIn}{Input}
\SetKwInput{KwOut}{Output}
\SetKwInput{KwParam}{Parameters}
\SetKwComment{tcp}{// }{}
\KwIn{RGB image $I$}
\KwParam{Depth model $\mathcal{D}$; (opt.) anomaly models $\{\mathcal{M}_i\}$; hysteresis/NMS thresholds; dynamic fusion $\varepsilon>0$; policy budget $K$; similarity penalty $\lambda\in[0,1]$; buffer thresholds $(\tau_{\text{high}},\tau_{\Delta})$; history feature set $\mathcal{H}$}
\KwOut{Selected targets $\mathcal{T}$; second-chance buffer $\mathcal{B}$; combined anomaly map $A_{\text{comb}}$; diagnostics}

\BlankLine
\tcp{(1) Preprocessing and depth}
$I_{\text{base}} \leftarrow \textsc{Enhance}(I)$\;
$D \leftarrow \mathcal{D}(I_{\text{base}})$ \tcp*[r]{monocular depth}
$I_{\text{dhe}} \leftarrow \textsc{DepthGuidedEnhance}(I_{\text{base}}, D)$\;

\tcp{(2) Multi-source anomaly maps}
$A_{\text{img}} \leftarrow \textsc{ImageCues}(I_{\text{dhe}})$\;
$A_{\text{depth}} \leftarrow \textsc{DepthCues}(D)$\;
$A_{\text{model}} \leftarrow \textsc{ModelCues}(\{\mathcal{M}_i\}, I_{\text{dhe}}, D)$ \tcp*[r]{AE/PaDiM/PatchCore optional}
$\{A_j\} \leftarrow \textsc{Normalize01}(\{A_{\text{img}},A_{\text{depth}},A_{\text{model}}\})$\;

\tcp{(3) Dynamic (entropy-weighted) fusion}
\ForEach{$A_j$}{
  $H_j \leftarrow \textsc{Entropy}(A_j)$\;
  $\tilde w_j \leftarrow \frac{1}{H_j+\varepsilon}$\;
}
$w_j \leftarrow \frac{\tilde w_j}{\sum_k \tilde w_k}$\;
$A_{\text{comb}} \leftarrow \sum_j w_j A_j$\;

\tcp{(4) Localization: candidate regions}
$\mathcal{R} \leftarrow \textsc{Localize}(A_{\text{comb}})$ \tcp*[r]{hysteresis + contours + NMS/IoU}

\tcp{(5) Region features, clustering, and curiosity score}
\ForEach{$r \in \mathcal{R}$}{
  $f_r \leftarrow \textsc{Latent}(r, I_{\text{dhe}})$ \tcp*[r]{crop + encoder features}
  $C(r) \leftarrow \textsc{CuriosityBase}(r, A_{\text{comb}}, D)$\;
  $s(r) \leftarrow \max_{h\in\mathcal{H}} \textsc{CosSim}(f_r, h)$ \tcp*[r]{similarity to history (opt.)}
  $C'(r) \leftarrow C(r)\cdot \big(1-\lambda\, s(r)\big)$ \tcp*[r]{soft penalty}
}
$\text{cid}(r) \leftarrow \textsc{Cluster}(\{f_r\})$ \tcp*[r]{K-Means / DBSCAN}

\tcp{(6) Operational selection: diversity + budget}
$\mathcal{T} \leftarrow \emptyset$\;
\ForEach{cluster $c$}{
  $r^\star \leftarrow \arg\max_{r:\text{cid}(r)=c} C'(r)$\;
  $\mathcal{T} \leftarrow \mathcal{T} \cup \{r^\star\}$\;
}
$\mathcal{T} \leftarrow \textsc{TopK}(\mathcal{T}, K, \text{key}=C')$\;

\tcp{(7) Second-chance buffer (Priority Buffer)}
$\mathcal{B} \leftarrow \{ r\in\mathcal{R}\,:\, C(r)>\tau_{\text{high}} \wedge (C(r)-C'(r))>\tau_{\Delta}\}$\;
$\mathcal{H} \leftarrow \textsc{UpdateHistory}(\mathcal{H}, \{f_r: r\in\mathcal{T}\})$\;

\Return $(\mathcal{T}, \mathcal{B}, A_{\text{comb}})$\;

\label{alg:artps}

Each layer is designed to be independently testable and profileable for runtime and memory.

\subsection{Input Enhancement}
To improve visual quality and strengthen anomaly/edge cues, we apply an automatic preprocessing chain: resolution normalization, noise reduction, contrast-limited adaptive histogram equalization (CLAHE), gamma balancing, and mild sharpening. Edge-preserving filters (edge-guided / context-aware) mitigate over-smoothing. These steps improve edge consistency for depth estimation and increase separability of anomaly components. Photometrically, we compute brightness- and saturation-based indicators to detect shadows/specular highlights for downstream suppression.

The image enhancement chain applies the following operations in sequence:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Resolution normalization:} For an input image \(I \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W \times 3}\), resize to a target resolution \(H' \times W'\) via bicubic interpolation:
\[ I' = \text{resize}(I, (H', W'), \text{method='bicubic'}) \]

\item \textbf{Denoising:} Edge-preserving smoothing via bilateral filtering:
\[ I_{\text{denoised}}(p) = \frac{1}{W_p} \sum_{q \in \mathcal{N}(p)} I(q) \cdot w_s(p,q) \cdot w_r(I(p), I(q)) \]
where \(w_s\) is the spatial weight, \(w_r\) is the range (color) weight, and \(W_p\) is the normalization constant.

\item \textbf{Contrast enhancement (CLAHE):} Local histogram equalization with clipping:
\[ I_{\text{enhanced}}(p) = \text{CLAHE}(I_{\text{denoised}}(p), \text{clip\_limit}=2.0, \text{tile\_grid\_size}=(8,8)) \]

\item \textbf{Gamma balancing:} Adaptive gamma correction:
\[ \gamma = \frac{\log(0.5)}{\log(\mu_I + \epsilon)}, \quad I_{\text{gamma}}(p) = I_{\text{enhanced}}(p)^{\gamma} \]
where \(\mu_I\) is the mean intensity and \(\epsilon\) is a small constant for numerical stability.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Depth-guided adaptive enhancement (Depth-Guided Enhancement)}
A key risk of standard photometric enhancement is that it can unnecessarily boost contrast in far-field regions (horizon/sky-like low-structure areas), producing \emph{spurious anomalies}. ARTPS reduces this effect by adapting enhancement using a near-field mask derived from the depth map: contrast enhancement and sharpening are applied strongly only to near-field pixels, and weakly (or disabled) in far-field regions. This improves near-target sensitivity while reducing far-field false positives.

Using a normalized depth map \(D_n(p)\in[0,1]\), we define a proximity weight:
\[
g(p)=\sigma\big(\alpha(\tau - D_n(p))\big),
\]
where \(\sigma(\cdot)\) is the sigmoid function, \(\tau\) is the near/far threshold, and \(\alpha\) controls transition sharpness. For any enhancement operator \(T(\cdot)\) (e.g., CLAHE, sharpening), the depth-guided composition is:
\[
I_{\text{dhe}}(p) = g(p)\, T(I(p)) + (1-g(p))\, I(p)
\]
This design can be reported as adaptive pre-processing aimed at false-positive suppression and evaluated via ablation (“DHE off/on”).

\subsection{Monocular depth estimation}
We obtain a dense depth map using a transformer-based monocular depth model. To preserve fine details we optionally apply: (i) edge-guided filtering, (ii) fast global smoothing, and (iii) weighted median filtering; (iv) test-time augmentation via horizontal flips combined by fusion. Depth-derived features include summary statistics (mean, variance, skewness, kurtosis), slope magnitude, surface-normal convergence, planarity, and roughness. To preserve far-field detail, we apply hysteresis-like thresholding in weakly structured regions guided by depth gradients.

Beyond anomaly fusion, monocular depth is operationally valuable because it separates \emph{geometry} from \emph{photometry}. This is critical for rover imagery where the robot body and its cast shadows can otherwise trigger false detections. In particular, rover structural parts (mast/chassis/wheels) typically form a near-field depth layer that is consistent across frames, enabling a \emph{self-mask} (geometry-based exclusion) and reducing self-triggered anomalies. Likewise, cast shadows can be strong in RGB cues but do not correspond to true depth discontinuities; depth consistency can therefore suppress shadow-driven false positives. Figure~\ref{fig:depth-rover}(b) illustrates how the rover body and surrounding terrain become separable in the depth-derived visualization.

Depth also provides a compact representation of \emph{terrain structure} for traverse support. Depth gradients and surface breaks indicate obstacles, rocks, and ledges; local roughness and slope statistics can support fine-grained path following cues (e.g., wheel-placement risk screening) by highlighting where the surface is uneven or rapidly changing in geometry. While ARTPS focuses on target prioritization rather than full navigation, these depth-derived cues are directly compatible with downstream traversability or wheel-footprint analysis modules.

The depth model follows a Vision Transformer (ViT) encoder--decoder architecture. For an input image \(I\), patch embedding and positional encoding yield:

\[ E = \text{PatchEmbed}(I) + \text{PositionalEncoding} \]

Patch embedding is computed as:
\[ \text{PatchEmbed}(I) = \text{Linear}(\text{Reshape}(I, (H \cdot W, P^2 \cdot C))) \]

where \(P\) is the patch size, \(C\) is the number of channels, and \(H,W\) are image dimensions.

Feature extraction uses multi-head self-attention:
\[ \text{Attention}(Q,K,V) = \text{softmax}\left(\frac{QK^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)V \]

Multi-head attention is defined as:
\[ \text{MultiHead}(Q,K,V) = \text{Concat}(\text{head}_1, \ldots, \text{head}_h)W^O \]

where each head is:
\[ \text{head}_i = \text{Attention}(QW_i^Q, KW_i^K, VW_i^V) \]

The depth map \(D \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W}\) is produced by decoder layers:
\[ D = \text{Decoder}(\text{Encoder}(E)) \]

The feed-forward network is:
\[ \text{FFN}(x) = W_2 \cdot \text{ReLU}(W_1 \cdot x + b_1) + b_2 \]

Depth refinement steps include:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Edge-guided filtering:} Depth edges \(E_D\) are refined using image edges \(E_I\):
\[ E_D'(p) = E_D(p) \cdot \exp(-\alpha \|E_I(p)\|_2) \]

\item \textbf{Global smoothing:} Enforce consistency via a Poisson equation:
\[ \nabla^2 D' = \nabla \cdot \nabla D \]

\item \textbf{Weighted median filter:} Edge-preserving smoothing with spatial weights:
\[ D_{\text{filtered}}(p) = \text{median}\{D(q) \cdot w(p,q) : q \in \mathcal{N}(p)\} \]
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Multi-component anomaly fusion}
The hybrid design integrates complementary cues to improve anomaly detection reliability. Rather than relying on a single signal, ARTPS combines multiple anomaly indicators:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Reconstruction difference:} Per-pixel error maps from an encoder--decoder representation; uncertainty-aware handling is applied for low-texture regions.
  \item \textbf{Image texture/edges:} Gradient magnitude, multi-scale Laplacian/DoG detail emphasis; inverse brightness and saturation indicators for shadow; low-saturation masking for specularity.
  \item \textbf{Depth discontinuities:} Surface breaks via depth gradients/Laplacian; proximity weighting for small near-field targets; planarity consistency via normal \(z\)-component convergence.
  \item \textbf{Advanced anomaly cues (optional):} Soft fusion of feature-statistics / patch-distribution methods and nearest-neighbor memory-bank maps (e.g., PaDiM/PatchCore) \cite{defard2021padim,roth2021towards}.
\end{itemize}

The fusion procedure proceeds as follows: (1) each cue is normalized to \([0,1]\), (2) maps are fused via weighted summation, (3) hysteresis thresholding and morphology produce binary candidate regions, and (4) candidate confidence is re-scaled using cross-cue consistency and depth/topography agreement.

This design supports \emph{diversity} (heterogeneous cues), \emph{reliability} (errors in one cue can be compensated by others), \emph{adaptivity} (weights can be tuned to field conditions), and \emph{robustness} to noise and uncertainty. Figure~\ref{fig:far-small-detail} illustrates the transition from a raw frame to the fused anomaly output.

Fusion is expressed as:

\[ A_{\text{combined}}(p) = \sum_{i=1}^{N} w_i \cdot A_i(p) \]

\subsubsection{Entropy-weighted dynamic fusion (Entropy-Weighted Fusion)}
Fixed \(w_i\) selection is vulnerable to reviewer criticism (“how were weights chosen?”). ARTPS therefore defines a \textbf{dynamic} weighting scheme based on Shannon entropy, which measures the instantaneous \emph{uncertainty} (distribution flatness) of each anomaly map. Intuitively, if an anomaly map is \emph{sharp} and \emph{selective} (low entropy), it is treated as more confident and receives higher weight; if it is diffuse (high entropy), it is down-weighted.

After normalizing each anomaly map \(A_i\) to the range \(A_i' \in [0,1]\), we compute the probability distribution \(p_{ik}\) using a \(K\)-bin histogram. The Shannon entropy is then calculated as:
\[
H_i = -\sum_{k=1}^{K} p_{ik}\log(p_{ik}+\epsilon)
\]
Dynamic weights are then defined as:
\[
\tilde{w}_i = \frac{1}{H_i+\epsilon},\qquad
w_i = \frac{\tilde{w}_i}{\sum_{j=1}^{N}\tilde{w}_j}
\]
and fusion is updated as \(A_{\text{combined}}(p)=\sum_i w_i A_i'(p)\). This mechanism makes ARTPS \emph{self-calibrating}; for example, under shadow/specular conditions, image-based cues may become diffuse (higher entropy), while depth discontinuity cues can remain sharper (lower entropy) and thus receive higher weight.

Here \(A_i(p)\) is the \(i\)-th anomaly component and \(w_i\) is its weight. Each component is normalized to \([0,1]\):

\[ A_i'(p) = \frac{A_i(p) - \min(A_i)}{\max(A_i) - \min(A_i)} \]

For shadow and specular suppression we use:
\[ A_{\text{shadow}}(p) = \exp\left(-\frac{(L(p) - \mu_L)^2}{2\sigma_L^2}\right) \]
\[ A_{\text{specular}}(p) = \exp\left(-\frac{(S(p) - \mu_S)^2}{2\sigma_S^2}\right) \]

where \(L(p)\) and \(S(p)\) denote brightness and saturation, respectively.

For PaDiM, anomaly scoring is based on the Mahalanobis distance:

\[ M(p) = \sqrt{(f_p - \mu)^T \Sigma^{-1} (f_p - \mu)} \]

where \(f_p\) is the feature vector at pixel \(p\), \(\mu\) is the mean feature vector of normal data, and \(\Sigma\) is the covariance matrix.

Autoencoder reconstruction error is:

\[ E_{\text{recon}}(p) = \|I(p) - \hat{I}(p)\|_2 \]

where \(I(p)\) is the original image and \(\hat{I}(p)\) is its reconstruction.

\subsection{Localization and box merging}
Contours yield axis-aligned and rotated bounding-box hypotheses. IoU-based suppression and center-proximity merging consolidate multi-scale clusters into a compact candidate set. Small-area thresholds are relaxed for far-field regions. Box geometry can be calibrated with local slope and normal-distribution cues to align with geological plausibility.

Localization follows:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Contour extraction:} Canny edge detection on the anomaly map:
\[ C = \text{Canny}(A_{\text{combined}}, \text{low\_threshold}, \text{high\_threshold}) \]

\item \textbf{Box hypotheses:} Minimum-area rectangles from contours:
\[ B = \text{minAreaRect}(C) \]

\item \textbf{IoU-based suppression:} For overlapping boxes:
\[ \text{IoU}(B_i, B_j) = \frac{|B_i \cap B_j|}{|B_i \cup B_j|} \]

\item \textbf{Box merging:} Based on center proximity:
\[ d(B_i, B_j) = \|c_i - c_j\|_2 < \text{threshold} \]
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Curiosity score}
Curiosity scoring is a central component of ARTPS and is defined as a linear combination of normalized signals: (i) \emph{known value} (classifier confidence), (ii) reconstruction-based anomaly, (iii) combined anomaly density, (iv) depth variance, and (v) roughness.

Weights are learned via regularized regression on rank-oriented validation data and can be adjusted to field conditions. Formally, for a candidate region \(r\),
\[ C(r) = w_1 \, S_{\text{known}}(r) + w_2 \, S_{\text{recon}}(r) + w_3 \, S_{\text{anom}}(r) + w_4 \, \sigma^2_{\text{depth}}(r) + w_5 \, R_{\text{rough}}(r), \]
where \(w_i \ge 0\) and optionally \(\sum_i w_i = 1\). Component scores are normalized via min--max scaling or robust \(z\)-scores. For operational safety, \(S_{\text{anom}}\) is penalized in regions overlapping with shadow/specular indicators.

Per-region component definitions:

\begin{align}
S_{\text{known}}(r) &= \frac{1}{|r|} \sum_{p \in r} P_{\text{classifier}}(p) \\
S_{\text{recon}}(r) &= \frac{1}{|r|} \sum_{p \in r} \|I(p) - \hat{I}(p)\|_2 \\
S_{\text{anom}}(r) &= \frac{1}{|r|} \sum_{p \in r} A_{\text{combined}}(p) \\
\sigma^2_{\text{depth}}(r) &= \frac{1}{|r|} \sum_{p \in r} (D(p) - \bar{D}_r)^2 \\
R_{\text{rough}}(r) &= \frac{1}{|r|} \sum_{p \in r} \|\nabla D(p)\|_2
\end{align}

where \(|r|\) is the number of pixels in region \(r\) and \(\bar{D}_r\) is the mean depth in \(r\).

\subsubsection{Diversity penalty and redundancy minimization (Diversity Penalty)}
A key operational risk is repeatedly selecting highly similar targets and consuming bandwidth/energy budgets on redundant observations. ARTPS therefore adds a diversity penalty for \emph{redundancy minimization}: if a new candidate is highly similar to recently selected targets in feature space, its policy score is reduced.

To decouple from pixel-level similarity, the representation is defined in a \textbf{latent space}. For each target region \(r\), we extract a latent vector from the autoencoder encoder bottleneck:
\[
z_r=\mathrm{Enc}(x_r)\in\mathbb{R}^{d_z},
\]
where \(x_r\) is the cropped input tensor for region \(r\) and \(\mathrm{Enc}(\cdot)\) is the encoder. Assuming this vector captures structural/morphological cues, similarity and diversity are computed from \(z_r\) (optionally concatenated with depth summaries \(\phi(r)=[z_r;\,g(r)]\)). At time \(t\), the maximum cosine similarity to the last \(m\) selections is
\[
s_t(r)=\max_{j\in\{t-m,\dots,t-1\}} \frac{z_r^\top z_{r_j}}{\|z_r\|_2\,\|z_{r_j}\|_2+\epsilon}
\]
and the penalized score is
\[
C'(r)=C(r)\cdot\big(1-\lambda\, s_t(r)\big),
\]
where \(\lambda\in[0,1]\) controls the penalty strength. This increases short-horizon target diversity and reduces lock-in to a single morphology.

\subsubsection{Feature-space clustering for multi-shape management (Feature-Space Clustering)}
The diversity penalty alone can make “similar vs. different” decisions depend on selection order. A stronger rover policy clusters candidates in latent feature space and selects \emph{the highest-scoring target from each cluster}. This enforces representation of different morphologies (e.g., round/angular/elongated) within a single pass and prevents lock-in to one shape type.

For a candidate set \(\mathcal{R}\) at a given time, we cluster latent vectors \(\{z_r\}_{r\in\mathcal{R}}\) using either K-Means (fixed \(K\)) or DBSCAN (data-driven number of clusters) \cite{macqueen1967kmeans,ester1996dbscan}. Let the cluster assignment be \(c(r)\in\{1,\dots,K\}\). The selection policy is:
\[
r_k^\star=\arg\max_{r\in\mathcal{R}:\,c(r)=k} C'(r),\qquad
\mathcal{T}=\{r_k^\star\}_{k=1}^{K}
\]
In practice, to respect a mission budget \(B\) (e.g., \(B=5\) targets), we keep the top \(B\) cluster representatives by \(C'(r_k^\star)\). This mechanism forms a decision layer that explicitly manages multiple shape types and can be quantified via ablation.

\subsection{Explainability and uncertainty}
The diagnostic panel reports per-region component contributions with short explanations and indexed labels. When signal strength is low or cross-component disagreement is high, an uncertainty indicator is increased and the operator is warned. These indicators facilitate re-inspection of triggered regions and adjustment of data-collection policy.

Uncertainty is computed as:
\[ U(r) = \sqrt{\frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} (S_i(r) - \bar{S}(r))^2} \]

where \(S_i(r)\) is the \(i\)-th component score and \(\bar{S}(r)\) is their mean in region \(r\). High uncertainty values are visually emphasized to draw operator attention.

\subsection{Metrics and evaluation}
We evaluate ARTPS using the following metrics:

\subsubsection{Anomaly detection metrics}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{AUROC (Area Under ROC):} area under the ROC curve
\item \textbf{AUPRC (Area Under PRC):} area under the Precision--Recall curve
\item \textbf{F1-Score:} harmonic mean of precision and recall
\item \textbf{False Positive Rate:} false-alarm rate
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Depth estimation metrics}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Relative Absolute Error:} \(\text{RAE} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \frac{|d_i - \hat{d}_i|}{d_i}\)
\item \textbf{Root Mean Square Error:} \(\text{RMSE} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} (d_i - \hat{d}_i)^2}\)
\item \textbf{Threshold accuracy:} fractions under \(\delta < 1.25, \delta < 1.25^2, \delta < 1.25^3\)
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Curiosity ranking metrics}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{nDCG (Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain):} ranking quality
\item \textbf{Spearman correlation:} rank correlation
\item \textbf{Kendall's Tau:} rank consistency
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Formula details}
For anomaly detection metrics:

\begin{align}
\text{Precision} &= \frac{\text{TP}}{\text{TP} + \text{FP}} \\
\text{Recall} &= \frac{\text{TP}}{\text{TP} + \text{FN}} \\
\text{F1-Score} &= 2 \cdot \frac{\text{Precision} \cdot \text{Recall}}{\text{Precision} + \text{Recall}} \\
\text{False Positive Rate} &= \frac{\text{FP}}{\text{FP} + \text{TN}}
\end{align}

where TP, FP, TN, and FN denote true positives, false positives, true negatives, and false negatives, respectively.

Additional depth formulas:

\begin{align}
\text{Mean Absolute Error (MAE)} &= \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} |d_i - \hat{d}_i| \\
\text{Log10 Error} &= \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} |\log_{10}(d_i) - \log_{10}(\hat{d}_i)| \\
\text{Threshold Accuracy} &= \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \max\left(\frac{d_i}{\hat{d}_i}, \frac{\hat{d}_i}{d_i}\right) < \delta
\end{align}

where \(d_i\) is ground-truth depth, \(\hat{d}_i\) is predicted depth, and \(\delta\) is the threshold.

For nDCG:

\begin{align}
\text{DCG@k} &= \sum_{i=1}^{k} \frac{2^{rel_i} - 1}{\log_2(i + 1)} \\
\text{nDCG@k} &= \frac{\text{DCG@k}}{\text{IDCG@k}}
\end{align}

where \(rel_i\) is the relevance of the item at position \(i\), and IDCG is the ideal DCG.

\subsection{Comparison methods (baselines)}
To meet reviewer expectations for comparability, we report ARTPS against three baselines:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{OpenCV (Texture/Edge):} a classical baseline that derives anomaly cues from RGB texture/edge indicators \cite{opencv2023}.
\item \textbf{Depth-only:} an anomaly baseline based solely on depth discontinuities/gradients from the monocular depth map.
\item \textbf{PaDiM/PatchCore:} widely used industrial anomaly baselines \cite{defard2021padim,roth2021towards}. We use \texttt{wide\_resnet50\_2} (WRN-50-2) as backbone.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Protocol:} We use identical train/validation/test splits, consistent random seeds, and (where applicable) the same preprocessing. Decision thresholds are selected on validation data; results are reported on the test set. We report AUROC, AUPRC, F1, and operational speed (FPS / per-image inference time).

\subsection{Optimization and hyperparameter tuning}
Hyperparameters are tuned via grid search and Bayesian optimization:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Depth model:} learning rate 1e-4, batch size 8, epochs 100
\item \textbf{Anomaly fusion:} weight factors \([0.3, 0.25, 0.25, 0.1, 0.1]\)
\item \textbf{Localization:} IoU threshold 0.5, minimum area 100 pixels
\item \textbf{Curiosity scoring:} regularization 0.01, validation split 0.2
\item \textbf{Clustering (feature space):} K-Means \(K=5\);\linebreak DBSCAN \(\varepsilon=0.35\), \texttt{min\_samples}=2 (default experimental settings).
\end{itemize}

The optimization objective maximizes a weighted sum of AUROC and nDCG:
\[ \text{Objective} = 0.6 \cdot \text{AUROC} + 0.4 \cdot \text{nDCG} \]

\section{Ablation and sensitivity analysis}
\textbf{Component impact:} We measure the individual and combined contributions of reconstruction/texture/depth channels and the effect of optional advanced anomaly maps on fusion. We report AUROC/AUPRC drops when each component is disabled and qualitative effects on representative scenes.

\textbf{Refinement steps:} We analyze how edge-guided filtering, global smoothing, weighted median filtering, and test-time augmentation affect fine detail and noise in depth. Smoothing without edge preservation can blur discontinuities, while over-sharpening may introduce spurious edges.

\textbf{Thresholds and weights:} We study how hysteresis, NMS-IoU, minimum-area, and shadow/specular suppression parameters affect sensitivity--specificity trade-offs. Regularizing curiosity weights reduces overfitting; increased far-field tolerance must be balanced to preserve small near-field sensitivity.

\textbf{Field variation:} We evaluate stability under low texture (rocky surfaces), moving shadows, and saturation artifacts. Integrated suppression and depth-discontinuity cues improve robustness under photometric fluctuations.

\textbf{Resource sensitivity:} Under tighter memory/compute budgets, advanced anomaly maps can be disabled while maintaining similar ranking stability with core components, offering a practical profile for edge deployment.

\subsection{Hardware performance profile}
We profile inference under realistic workloads and target real-time feasibility on constrained hardware. The hybrid design supports graceful degradation (e.g., disabling optional advanced cues) when resource budgets are tight.

Specifically, in a lightweight configuration (OpenCV-based preprocessing + fusion + localization, excluding learned depth/AE inference), the core pipeline achieves \(\approx 28.1\) FPS (\(\approx 35.6\) ms/image) at 256\(\times\)256, \(\approx 12.8\) FPS (\(\approx 78.1\) ms/image) at 384\(\times\)384, and \(\approx 4.0\) FPS (\(\approx 250.0\) ms/image) at 768\(\times\)768 on our development workstation, supporting real-time onboard target screening in edge-oriented settings.

\textbf{Practical acceleration strategies:} Beyond raw throughput, mission software benefits from predictable latency and controllable compute budgets. ARTPS therefore exposes configuration knobs that allow operators to trade accuracy for speed in a principled way. In practice, we recommend:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Resolution budgeting:} Use 256\(\times\)256 or 384\(\times\)384 for onboard screening; reserve higher resolutions for deferred analysis or second-chance buffer re-evaluation.
\item \textbf{Disable optional heavy cues when needed:} Turn off advanced learned anomaly maps (PaDiM/PatchCore) and run the core fusion stack first; re-enable them only for high-priority frames or regions of interest.
\item \textbf{Reduce depth refinement and test-time augmentation:} If monocular depth is enabled, disable test-time augmentation (e.g., flip-TTA) and expensive post-processing/refinement stages when strict latency is required; enable them only when geometry quality is critical.
\item \textbf{Mixed precision and graph compilation:} On compatible GPUs, use mixed precision (FP16/AMP) and compilation (e.g., \texttt{torch.compile}) for neural components to reduce latency while preserving numerical stability.
\item \textbf{Deployment-oriented backends:} For edge devices, export neural submodules (depth/encoder) to ONNX and use hardware-specific runtimes (e.g., TensorRT) when available; keep OpenCV stages on CPU/GPU according to the platform's memory bandwidth.
\item \textbf{Policy-level throttling:} Reduce the decision budget \(K\), lower the number of candidate boxes, and tune NMS/IoU thresholds to bound the worst-case number of regions processed per frame.
\end{itemize}

\section{Limitations and future work}
Monocular depth estimation cannot recover absolute scene scale; far-field regions require normalization and scale-aware thresholding. In very bright or low-contrast areas, shadow and specular indicators can overlap and occasionally introduce spurious cues. While real-time feasibility is maintained with a lightweight edge-oriented configuration, fusing advanced anomaly maps can increase memory usage; ARTPS therefore supports profile-aware fallbacks.

Future work includes tighter alignment with geo-consistent products (e.g., orthophoto/DEM consistency), uncertainty modeling at both component and decision levels, and joint optimization with active exploration policies. Incorporating multimodal signals (e.g., multispectral photometry, spectral ratios) is also expected to improve contextual awareness of the curiosity policy.

\subsection{False-negative management and priority buffer (Priority Buffer)}
A critical reviewer question is: “Can your system suppress and miss a \emph{high-value} target due to visual/morphological similarity?” ARTPS avoids \textbf{hard filtering} in diversity and clustering decisions; instead, it uses a \emph{soft penalty} and a \textbf{priority buffer} to mitigate false negatives.

For each candidate region we maintain two scores: (i) the raw score \(C(r)\) and (ii) the policy score \(C'(r)\). Targets that remain high in raw score but are substantially reduced by similarity/diversity policy are placed into a secondary queue:
\[
\Delta(r)=C(r)-C'(r),\qquad
r\in\mathcal{B}\ \ \text{if}\ \ C(r)>\tau_{\text{high}}\ \wedge\ \Delta(r)>\tau_{\Delta}.
\]
Here \(\mathcal{B}\) is the priority buffer, \(\tau_{\text{high}}\) is a “potential high-value” threshold, and \(\tau_{\Delta}\) captures a “meaningful drop due to policy” threshold. When uncertainty \(U(r)\) is high (challenging/unstable field conditions), the buffer rule can be widened to further reduce false-negative risk.

\textbf{Second-chance policy:} After executing the primary target set \(\mathcal{T}\), if remaining time/energy allows (or if primary targets yield less than expected), the buffer \(\mathcal{B}\) is re-evaluated and targets with the highest raw score \(C(r)\) are scheduled. This strengthens operational safety by reducing the chance of discarding high-value anomalies while still pursuing diversity.

By integrating monocular depth estimation with multi-component anomaly fusion and learnable curiosity scoring, the system improves prioritization quality while addressing operational safety concerns via diversity control and a second-chance buffer.

The architecture is modular and amenable to profiling under constrained resources. Safety-oriented design choices (fallback modes, uncertainty indicators, and buffer-based false-negative mitigation) support field readiness and operator trust. This work is a step toward more autonomous and reliable planetary exploration workflows.
